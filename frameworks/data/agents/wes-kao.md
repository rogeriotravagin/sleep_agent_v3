  state_change_method:
    name: "The State Change Method - Delivering Engaging Live Sessions"
    category: "engagement"
    origin: "Wes Kao - altMBA/Maven (2021)"
    frequency: "Every live session design"
    command: "*state-change"

    principle: |
      "Students don't owe you their attention. Monotony causes audiences to tune out.
      Movement causes audiences to become alert. A 'state change' is anything that
      punctuates an instructor's monologue. Aim for a state change every 3-5 minutes."

    why_it_works: |
      Humans are alert to change. Shiny pieces of foil catch our eyes because
      they are different from their surroundings. The same principle applies to
      teaching: when you change the modality, students perk up.

      Zoom fatigue is real because we sit still, stare ahead, and maintain eye
      contact with a screen for long stretches. State changes break that monotony.

    types_of_state_changes:
      verbal_shifts:
        - name: "Change speaker"
          example: "Bring on a guest, hand off to co-instructor, spotlight a student"
        - name: "Change energy"
          example: "Shift from lecture to storytelling, speed up or slow down pace"
        - name: "Ask a question"
          example: "Don't just rhetorical - actually pause and wait for response"

      participation_shifts:
        - name: "Chat engagement"
          example: "'Type your answer in chat' - creates physical action"
        - name: "Poll/quiz"
          example: "Use Zoom polls, Slido, or Mentimeter for quick checks"
        - name: "Hand raise"
          example: "'Raise your hand if you've experienced this' - visible participation"
        - name: "Unmute moment"
          example: "Have everyone unmute and react at the same time"

      grouping_shifts:
        - name: "Breakout rooms"
          example: "2-4 person discussions for 5-10 minutes"
        - name: "Pair work"
          example: "Turn to your neighbor/buddy - works even on Zoom"
        - name: "Solo reflection"
          example: "Take 2 minutes to write your thoughts - then share"
        - name: "Gallery walk"
          example: "Review others' work in shared doc or Miro board"

      content_shifts:
        - name: "Visual change"
          example: "New slide, switch to whiteboard, show demo"
        - name: "Medium change"
          example: "From slides to video, from talking to live demo"
        - name: "Perspective change"
          example: "From theory to case study, from concept to example"
        - name: "Activity type"
          example: "From listening to doing, from individual to group"

    session_design_template:
      duration: "60 minutes"
      structure:
        minute_00_05:
          activity: "Welcome + Agenda"
          state_change: "Start with an engaging hook question"
        minute_05_12:
          activity: "Concept introduction (lecture)"
          state_change: "Pause at minute 8 for chat response"
        minute_12_22:
          activity: "Breakout rooms - Apply concept"

        minute_22_30:
          activity: "Share back from breakouts"

        minute_30_35:

  # â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•
  super_specific_how:
    name: "The Super Specific How - Making Courses Rigorous and Actionable"
    category: "content"
    origin: "Wes Kao - Maven (2021)"
    frequency: "Every content development session"
    command: "*super-specific-how"

    principle: |
      "Mediocre courses focus on the WHAT. Great courses focus on the HOW.
      Most instructors spend 80% on the what/why, and only 20% on the how.
      Great instructors do the opposite: They spend 80% of time on the how.
      This is why their courses feel meaty and substantial."

    the_problem_with_vague: |
      Students often think: "Well, that wasn't worth my time. I already knew most of it."
      Why? Because instructors give high-level advice that students can find anywhere.
      Vague advice = students nod along but don't change behavior.
      Specific how = students have a playbook they can follow.

    vague_vs_specific_examples:
      example_1:
        topic: "Communication"
        vague: "Use warm yet authoritative language in your emails"
        specific: "Start with 'I noticed that...' instead of 'You always...' - it's specific and observational rather than accusatory"

      example_2:
        topic: "Negotiation"
        vague: "Always be willing to walk away"
        specific: "Say 'I need to think about this' to create space. If they pressure you, respond 'I want to make sure we're both getting a good deal' - this reframes walking away as mutual benefit"

      example_3:
        topic: "Course Design"
        vague: "Make your course more interactive"
        specific: "Add a chat prompt every 7 minutes. Phrase it as 'Type your answer before I reveal mine' - this commits them publicly and creates anticipation"

      example_4:
        topic: "Presentation"
        vague: "Tell stories to engage your audience"
        specific: "Use the 'Specific Moment' opener: Start with 'I was sitting in my office at 9:47pm on a Tuesday when...' - the specificity makes it feel real and puts the audience in the scene"

    how_to_create_super_specific_how:
      step_1:
        name: "Start with the transformation"
        action: "What behavior do you want students to change?"
        example: "From: Students hesitate to share ideas in meetings. To: Students confidently share ideas using a specific framing technique."

      step_2:
        name: "Identify the 'stuck point'"
        action: "Where do students typically get stuck?"
        example: "They know they should share, but don't know the exact words to use."

      step_3:
        name: "Provide the literal script"
        action: "Give them the exact words, framework, or template"
        example: "'Here's a phrase I want you to try: Instead of saying 'I think we should...' say 'One option is to... The reason I like this approach is...' - it positions your idea as one option while still showing conviction."

      step_4:
        name: "Show the before/after"
        action: "Demonstrate the difference in real scenarios"
        example: "Before: 'Maybe we could try a different approach?' After: 'One option is to restructure the team into pods. The reason I like this is it reduces coordination overhead.'"

      step_5:
        name: "Provide practice scenarios"
        action: "Give students situations to apply the Super Specific How"
        example: "Try this in your next team meeting. Pick one idea you've been hesitant to share. Use the 'One option is...' frame. Report back to your cohort."

    content_audit_checklist:
      - question: "Can students DO something with this content immediately?"
        if_no: "Add a concrete action they can take"
      - question: "Is there a specific script, template, or framework?"
        if_no: "Create one - students need the literal words"
      - question: "Did I show the before/after?"
        if_no: "Demonstrate transformation with real examples"
      - question: "Will students say 'I already knew that'?"
        if_yes: "Go deeper - what's the non-obvious nuance?"
      - question: "Could I make this more specific?"
        always_yes: "There's almost always a more specific version"

    anti_patterns:
      - pattern: "Stating importance without showing application"
        example: "'It's important to be authentic in your communication'"
        fix: "Show specific phrases that signal authenticity"
      - pattern: "Using abstract concepts without concrete examples"
        example: "'Build psychological safety in your team'"
        fix: "Give 3 specific actions that create safety"
      - pattern: "Telling without showing"
        example: "'Practice active listening'"
        fix: "Demo active listening, show non-examples, provide practice scenarios"

  spiky_point_of_view:
    name: "Spiky Point of View - Standing Out in a Noisy World"
    category: "positioning"
    origin: "Wes Kao - Newsletter (2020)"
    frequency: "Personal branding, course positioning, content strategy"
    command: "*spiky-pov"

    principle: |
      "We live in a noisy world. Thousands of people are trying to get noticed.
      Unless you distinguish yourself, you'll never get a chance to show how different you are.
      To stand out, you need to develop a 'spiky' point of view - a perspective others can disagree with."

    what_is_spiky_pov: |
      A spiky point of view is:
      - A perspective others CAN disagree with
      - A belief you feel strongly about and are willing to advocate for
      - Your thesis about topics in your realm of expertise

      It's NOT:
      - A generic statement everyone agrees with
      - A safe, wishy-washy position
      - A rehash of conventional wisdom

    flat_vs_spiky_examples:
      example_1:
        topic: "Online Education"
        flat: "Online learning is the future of education"
        spiky: "MOOCs failed because they optimized for content when they should have optimized for completion. Content is no longer king - cohorts are."

      example_2:
        topic: "Course Pricing"
        flat: "Price your course based on the value it provides"
        spiky: "Underpricing your course hurts students. A $50 course has an 8% completion rate. A $2000 course has 95%. Price creates commitment."

      example_3:
        topic: "Management"
        flat: "Good managers give feedback"
        spiky: "Most feedback is lazy. If you're not giving scripts and showing the exact words to use, you're not actually helping."

      example_4:
        topic: "Career"
        flat: "It's important to manage up"
        spiky: "You're not being micromanaged - you're not communicating enough. Over-communication might be the right amount."

    how_to_develop_spiky_pov:
      step_1:
        name: "List your beliefs"
        action: "Write down 20+ things you believe about your field"
        prompt: "What do I believe that most people in my industry don't?"

      step_2:
        name: "Find the disagree-able ones"
        action: "For each belief, ask 'Would smart people disagree?'"
        test: "If everyone agrees, it's not spiky"

      step_3:
        name: "Sharpen the edge"
        action: "Make the statement more specific and provocative"
        technique: "Use contrast: 'Most people think X, but actually Y'"

      step_4:
        name: "Back it up"
        action: "Prepare evidence, examples, and logic"
        requirement: "Spiky doesn't mean unsupported - have the receipts"

      step_5:
        name: "Publish and iterate"
        action: "Share your spiky POV and see how people react"
        goal: "Some agreement, some disagreement = perfect"

    fifteen_spiky_pov_examples_from_wes:
      - "Content is no longer king - cohorts are."
      - "Students don't owe you their attention."
      - "Underpricing hurts your students."
      - "Accountability is a feature, not a bug."

      - "Premium pricing creates commitment that creates completion."
      - "The best courses focus on transformation, not information."
      - "Community is the moat - it's the only thing that can't be copied."
      - "Rigorous thinking is the antidote to lazy consensus."

    application_to_courses:
      positioning: "Your spiky POV differentiates your course from competitors"
      marketing: "Spiky takes create shareable content that spreads"
      content: "Build curriculum around your spiky POVs as core theses"
      community: "Attract students who resonate with your POV"

  # â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•
  rigorous_thinking:
    name: "Rigorous Thinking Framework - No Lazy Thinking"
    category: "communication"
    origin: "Wes Kao - Newsletter (2018, updated 2023)"
    frequency: "Team management, executive communication"
    command: "*rigorous-thinking"

    principle: |
      "The best managers teach their team how to think strategically. But smart people
      sometimes accidentally traumatize their teams. Every time someone has a question,
      you just answer it. Or worse, you give them a wrist slap for bringing a new idea.
      Neither builds rigorous thinkers."

    lazy_thinking_patterns:
      pattern_1:
        name: "Questions without hypotheses"
        lazy: "'What do you think we should do?'"
        rigorous: "'I think we should do X because Y. Does this make sense?'"

      pattern_2:
        name: "Insights without recommendations"
        lazy: "'I noticed our conversion rate dropped.'"
        rigorous: "'Our conversion rate dropped 15%. I think it's because of X. I recommend we try Y. Here's my 3-step plan.'"

      pattern_3:
        name: "Problems without solutions"
        lazy: "'This is broken.'"
        rigorous: "'This is broken. Here are three options to fix it. I recommend option 2 because...'"

      pattern_4:
        name: "Vague language"
        lazy: "'This could be better.'"
        rigorous: "'Specifically, paragraph 3 is unclear. I suggest changing X to Y because Z.'"

    hierarchy_of_contributions:
      level_1:
        name: "Insight"
        description: "Observation or data point"
        example: "'Our NPS dropped from 45 to 32.'"
        value: "Starting point, but low value alone"

      level_2:
        name: "Suggestion"
        description: "Idea without strong conviction"
        example: "'Maybe we should survey customers?'"
        value: "Helpful but non-committal"

      level_3:
        name: "Assertion"
        description: "Point of view with reasoning"
        example: "'Our NPS dropped because of the new onboarding flow. Here's why I think that and what we should do about it.'"
        value: "Most valuable - shows ownership and thinking"

    why_a_players_make_assertions:
      reasoning: |
        "Making assertions is scary because you might be wrong. But that's exactly
        why it's valuable. You're putting your reputation on the line. You're
        showing you've done the thinking. You're giving others something to react to.

        Leaders don't have time to do everyone's thinking for them. They need
        people who bring answers, not just questions."

    building_rigorous_thinking_culture:
      manager_actions:
        - "When someone brings a question, ask 'What do you think?' before answering"
        - "Praise assertions, even if they're wrong - 'I love that you took a stance'"
        - "Model rigorous thinking in your own communication"
        - "Create safety for being wrong - celebrate learning from mistakes"

      individual_actions:
        - "Always bring a hypothesis with your question"
        - "State your recommendation before asking for input"
        - "Show your work - explain your reasoning"
        - "Use specific language, not vague generalizations"

    anti_patterns:
      - "Asking questions just to have participation"
      - "Hedging everything with 'maybe' and 'I think'"
      - "Waiting for the boss to decide everything"
      - "Bringing problems without proposed solutions"
      - "Using vague words that could mean anything"

    principle: |
      "Communities are a focal point of CBCs. You can't just have a start and end date

      and stick through learning hard things. But course communities have unique challenges."

    unique_challenges:
      challenge_1:
        name: "Time-bounded trust building"
        problem: "Courses have a start and end date - trust must build quickly"
        solution: |
          - Curate for chemistry from the start
          - Create vulnerability early (intro exercises)
          - Small group formats accelerate connection
          - Instructor models openness first

      challenge_2:
        name: "Learning vs socializing balance"
        problem: "Too much socializing dilutes learning; too little creates isolation"
        solution: |
          - Learning activities that require collaboration
          - Social time is bonus, not core
          - Channel structure separates social from work
          - Clear norms about what goes where

      challenge_3:
        name: "Active vs passive members"
        problem: "Lurkers can drag down energy for active participants"
        solution: |
          - Required participation minimums
          - Small groups ensure everyone contributes
          - Peer feedback makes participation visible
          - Celebrate contribution, not just consumption

      challenge_4:
        name: "Alumni transition"
        problem: "What happens when the course ends?"
        solution: |
          - Clear expectations about post-course access
          - Alumni-only events and content
          - Graduated roles (mentors, guest speakers)
          - Referral programs keep alumni engaged

    community_architecture:
      channels:
        required:
          - name: "General/Announcements"

          - name: "Introductions"
