  # FRAMEWORK 1: THE SIMPLE WRITING SYSTEM
  # â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•
  simple_writing_system:
    name: "The Simple Writing System"
    category: "copywriting_methodology"
    origin: "John Carlton - SimpleWritingSystem.com"
    command: "*sws"

    philosophy: |
      Nothing good will ever happen in your business until your sales message
      gets written. And the best sales messages are the ones anyone can understand.

      You don't need fancy words. You don't need to be clever. You need to be CLEAR.
      Clear writing comes from clear thinking. If you can't explain it simply,
      you don't understand it well enough.

    core_principle: |
      WRITE LIKE YOU TALK. If you wouldn't say it to a friend at a bar,
      don't write it in your ad. The moment you start "writing" (as opposed
      to "communicating"), you lose your prospect.

    three_pillars:
      pillar_1:
        name: "One Idea Per Sentence"
        principle: "Each sentence delivers exactly ONE thought"
        instruction: |
          Read your sentence out loud. If you need to take a breath in the middle,
          split it. If there's an "and" connecting two complete thoughts, split it.
          Complexity is the enemy of clarity.
        example:
          bad: "Our revolutionary system combines cutting-edge technology with time-tested principles to deliver results that will transform your business while saving you time and money in ways you never thought possible."
          good: |
            Our system is revolutionary.
            It combines cutting-edge tech with time-tested principles.
            The results will transform your business.
            You'll save time. You'll save money.
            In ways you never thought possible.

      pillar_2:
        name: "Conversational Tone"
        principle: "Write like you're talking to ONE person at a bar"
        instruction: |
          Use contractions (you're, it's, don't).
          Use informal language.
          Use fragments. Like this one.
          Address them directly (you, your).
          Never sound like a corporation.
        test: |
          Read it out loud. Does it sound like something you'd actually SAY?
          If you stumble, rewrite. If it sounds stiff, loosen it up.
        example:
          bad: "One may find that implementing these strategies could potentially yield beneficial outcomes."
          good: "Look, when you use these strategies, you're gonna see results. Period."

      pillar_3:
        name: "Lizard Brain First"
        principle: "Hit their primal desires BEFORE engaging their logical mind"
        instruction: |
          The lizard brain (survival instincts) decides in milliseconds.
          Make them FEEL something before you explain anything.
          Fear, greed, lust, vanity, sloth - these drive action.
        lizard_brain_triggers:
          survival: "Protect yourself from [threat]"
          comfort: "Finally, an easy way to [desired outcome]"
          sex_appeal: "Become irresistible to [target]"
          status: "Join the elite few who [achievement]"
          curiosity: "The weird secret behind [intriguing phenomenon]"

    process:
      step_1:
        name: "Brain Dump"
        action: "Write everything about the product/service - no editing"
        tip: "Pretend you're explaining it to a confused friend. Get it ALL out."

      step_2:
        name: "Find the Hook"
        action: "Identify the ONE thing that makes this compelling"
        method: |
          Ask: "What's the one detail that made YOU interested?"
          Look for: The unexpected fact. The personal story. The weird angle.
          The 'one-legged golfer' is always hiding in there somewhere.

      step_3:
        name: "Write the First Draft"
        action: "Write fast, don't edit, follow the energy"
        tip: "Set a timer. Write until it beeps. No backspacing allowed."

      step_4:
        name: "Simplify Ruthlessly"
        action: "Cut every word that doesn't earn its place"
        checklist:
          - "Can I cut this word without losing meaning? CUT IT."
          - "Is this sentence more than one idea? SPLIT IT."
          - "Would I say this out loud? If not, REWRITE IT."
          - "Is there a simpler word? USE IT."

      step_5:
        name: "Read Aloud Test"
        action: "Read the entire piece out loud"
        criteria: |
          If you stumble, rewrite.
          If you run out of breath, shorten.
          If it sounds like 'writing', make it sound like talking.

      step_6:
        name: "The Pint Test"
        action: "Could you explain this in the time it takes to drink a beer?"
        principle: "If your message can't survive a casual conversation, it's too complicated."

  # â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•
  hook_mining_system:
    name: "The One-Legged Golfer Hook-Mining System"
    category: "hook_discovery"
    origin: "John Carlton's most famous ad story"
    command: "*find-hook"

    origin_story: |
      Carlton was writing a golf ad. The client was droning on about swing
      mechanics, weight transfer, blah blah blah. Then, almost as an afterthought,
      he mentioned: "Oh yeah, I developed this swing after watching a one-legged
      man hit the most perfect drive I'd ever seen."

      Carlton almost jumped out of his chair. THAT was the hook. Not the
      technical details. The one-legged golfer. It took nearly an hour to
      pull this 'little factoid' out of the expert - to him, it was old news.

      The resulting headline became legendary:
      "Amazing Secret Discovered By One-Legged Golfer Adds 50 Yards To Your
      Drives, Eliminates Hooks and Slices... And Can Slash Up To 10 Strokes
      From Your Game Almost Overnight!"

    philosophy: |
      The best hook is almost NEVER what the client thinks is important.
      It's buried in some casual aside, some throw-away comment, some
      detail they've stopped noticing because it's old news to them.

      Your job is to be the OUTSIDER who recognizes the gold.

    mining_process:
      step_1:
        name: "Deep Interview"
        action: "Talk to the expert/owner for AT LEAST an hour"
        questions:
          - "How did you discover this?"
          - "What was the moment you knew this worked?"
          - "What's the weirdest result you've ever seen?"
          - "Tell me about your most skeptical customer who became a believer"
          - "What's something about this that surprises even you?"
          - "What do your competitors NOT understand about this?"
          - "What's a detail you usually don't mention because it seems obvious?"

      step_2:
        name: "Listen for Throwaway Lines"
        action: "Pay attention when they say 'oh, by the way' or 'this is funny but...'"
        principle: |
          The hook is often in the disclaimer, the aside, the joke.
          When their voice changes - gets excited, gets embarrassed,
          gets nostalgic - THAT'S where the gold is.

      step_3:
        name: "The Outsider Test"
        action: "What would make a stranger at a party lean in and say 'Wait, what?'"
        criteria:
          - "Does it create instant curiosity?"
          - "Is it unexpected or counter-intuitive?"
          - "Does it have a visual element (one-legged, 180 pounds, 3 days)?"
          - "Does it promise a specific, desirable outcome?"

      step_4:
        name: "The Specificity Upgrade"
        action: "Make the hook as specific as possible"
        example:
          generic: "Learn to hit longer drives"
          specific: "Add 50 yards to your drives"
          hook_specific: "Amazing secret discovered by one-legged golfer adds 50 yards"

    hook_types:
      story_hook:
        description: "A compelling narrative element"
        examples:
          - "The one-legged golfer"
          - "The monk who made millions"
          - "The fired secretary who built an empire"

      discovery_hook:
        description: "A breakthrough or revelation"
        examples:
          - "Amazing secret discovered by..."
          - "What scientists accidentally found..."
          - "The weird trick that..."

      contradiction_hook:
        description: "Something that defies expectations"
        examples:
          - "Why working LESS made me MORE money"
          - "The lazy man's way to riches"
          - "How a chain-smoker outlived all his friends"

      specificity_hook:
        description: "Precise numbers and details"
        examples:
          - "Add 50 yards"
          - "Slash 10 strokes"
          - "In 17 days"

  # â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•â•
  aggressive_clarity_edit:
    name: "The Aggressive Clarity Edit"
    category: "editing"
    origin: "Carlton's ruthless editing methodology"
    command: "*simplify"

    philosophy: |
      First drafts are always too long, too clever, and too confusing.
      Your job in editing is to ATTACK every sentence like it owes you money.

      Every word must EARN its place. If it doesn't add to clarity or
      persuasion, it's dead weight. Kill it.

    editing_passes:
      pass_1:
        name: "The Word Assassin"
        action: "Cut every unnecessary word"
        targets:
          - "Very, really, quite, somewhat, rather"
          - "In order to (just say 'to')"
          - "The fact that (just state the fact)"
          - "I think, I believe, in my opinion (just say it)"
          - "That (often deletable)"
        example:
          before: "I really think that it's very important for you to understand that this is quite possibly the most effective system."
          after: "This is the most effective system."

      pass_2:
        name: "The Sentence Splitter"
        action: "Break long sentences into short ones"
        rule: "If it has more than one idea, split it"
        example:
          before: "Our revolutionary new system combines cutting-edge technology with time-tested principles to deliver results that will transform your business."
          after: |
            Our system is revolutionary.
            It combines cutting-edge tech with time-tested principles.
            The results will transform your business.

      pass_3:
        name: "The Jargon Killer"
        action: "Replace every fancy word with a simple one"
        replacements:
          utilize: "use"
          implement: "do"
          facilitate: "help"
          leverage: "use"
          synergy: "working together"
          paradigm: "way of thinking"
          optimize: "improve"
          actionable: "useful"

      pass_4:
        name: "The Read-Aloud Test"
        action: "Read every sentence out loud"
        criteria:
          - "Did you stumble? Rewrite."
          - "Did you run out of breath? Shorten."
          - "Does it sound like something you'd actually say? If not, rewrite."

      pass_5:
        name: "The Friend-at-a-Bar Test"
        action: "Imagine explaining this to a friend at a bar"
        question: "Would you say it this way? Or would they look at you like you're crazy?"

    simplicity_checklist:
      - "[ ] Every sentence has ONE idea"
      - "[ ] No sentence is longer than 20 words (ideally under 15)"
      - "[ ] No jargon or fancy words"
      - "[ ] Uses contractions (you're, it's, don't)"
      - "[ ] Reads smoothly out loud"
      - "[ ] A 12-year-old could understand it"

## THE ONE-LEGGED GOLFER STORY

This is Carlton's most famous example of hook mining, and it perfectly illustrates his approach.

**The Setup:**
Carlton was writing an ad for a golf product. The client was explaining the technical details of the swing - weight transfer, hip rotation, all the mechanical stuff that bored Carlton to tears.

**The Moment:**
Then, almost as an afterthought, the client mentioned: "Oh yeah, I developed this swing after watching a one-legged man hit the most perfect drive I'd ever seen at the driving range."

**Carlton's Reaction:**
He almost jumped out of his chair. THAT was the hook. Not the technical specs. The one-legged golfer.

**The Lesson:**
The best hooks are:
1. Hidden in throwaway comments
2. Unexpected or counter-intuitive
3. Visual and specific
4. Often dismissed by the client as "obvious" or "old news"

**The Headline:**
> "Amazing Secret Discovered By One-Legged Golfer Adds 50 Yards To Your Drives, Eliminates Hooks and Slices... And Can Slash Up To 10 Strokes From Your Game Almost Overnight!"
